UX & Conversion Audit · May 2026 · v2

My CookBooks needs a checkout button — and a felt reason to use it.

The product is well built and on-brand. The tier structure is sound. The funnel has two structural breaks that account for most of the missing paid revenue. Everything else is polish.

Scope: anonymous landing → register → free use → upgrade Source: dougis-org/cookbook-tanstack@main Files reviewed: 14 routes + lib

TL;DR — the three things that matter

  1. Ship checkout. /change-tier currently reads "Plan changes coming soon." There is no way for any user to pay, anywhere in the app. Everything else in this document is downstream of fixing this.
  2. Make free-tier ads actually felt. The first paid tier (Prep Cook, $27.99/yr) exists to remove ads — that's its single differentiating value. But ad-policy.ts only renders ads on public-marketing / public-content pages, so once a Home Cook signs in they never see one. Tier 2 sells a relief the user has never experienced. Either serve ads on free-tier app pages (so removal feels meaningful) or repackage what Prep Cook unlocks.
  3. Sell the product on the landing page. The hero says "CookBook" (wrong brand name), and the three feature cards all describe the same thing: browsing. There is no mention of saving, organising, importing, sharing, or printing — the things people would actually pay for.
  4. Also worth a hard look: Import is locked at the $99.99/yr top tier and Private recipes at $59.99/yr. Both are strong acquisition hooks; consider whether pulling either down (especially Import) would lift conversion more than they currently earn at the top.

The tier 2 problem

Prep Cook ($27.99/yr) is the first paid step, and its single value prop is removing ads — that's a reasonable, well-priced first rung for a content-heavy product. The issue isn't the tier; it's that free users never experience the thing they'd be paying to remove.

Looking at src/lib/ad-policy.ts: 

const AD_ENABLED_ROLES: PageRole[] = ['public-marketing', 'public-content']

Ads render only on the public landing + public-content routes — not on /home, /recipes, recipe detail, or any other authed-user surface. A Home Cook who saves 10 recipes and uses the app daily literally never sees an ad. So the Prep Cook upgrade is selling a relief from something they've never felt.

Two ways to fix it, in rough order of leverage:

Option A — deliver the pain (recommended)

Add a single banner ad slot to free-tier authed pages: /home, /recipes (above the grid), and recipe detail (between ingredients and steps, or at the bottom). Keep the slot quiet — one unit, not three — so free still feels usable. This validates the existing pricing structure: Prep Cook becomes "buy back the clean view of the product you already know." That's a measurable, daily-friction-driven upgrade trigger.

Expand AD_ENABLED_ROLES to include authenticated-home and authenticated-task; showUserAds() already correctly returns true for home-cook, so the gating is already right — the policy just isn't being applied to the pages where it would matter.

Option B — repackage Prep Cook

If you'd rather not serve ads in-app, Prep Cook needs a second tangible feature to justify the price. Candidates: print exports (if currently free, gate it), basic recipe import via URL (today only at top tier), cookbook PDF download, or recipe sharing links. Pick the cheapest-to-build one that has the highest "oh I'd use that" rating.

Worth considering separately: Import + Private placement

Independent of the ads question — Import is currently the highest-tier feature ($99.99/yr). It's also the most search-friendly acquisition hook ("how do I import my recipes from X"). Top-tier gating is defensible (limits abuse, anchors the premium price) but you should decide deliberately: is Import a premium feature, or an acquisition feature? Same question for Private recipes at Sous Chef ($59.99/yr). Both decisions are upstream of the pricing page redesign in F9.

Findings, ranked by impact ÷ effort

F1 Checkout doesn't exist
Severity
Fatal
Where
src/routes/change-tier.tsx renders "Plan changes coming soon."
Why it matters
There is no path from "want to pay" to "have paid." Every other recommendation in this document is moot until this is fixed.
Recommendation
Wire up Stripe checkout (src/lib/stripe.ts already exists). Minimum viable flow: tier card "Upgrade" → Stripe Checkout session → webhook updates user.tier → success page lands user back on /account with new entitlements. Annual + monthly price IDs per tier.
F2 Prep Cook's value (no ads) is invisible to the people meant to buy it
Severity
Fatal
Where
src/lib/ad-policy.ts — ads only render on public-marketing + public-content roles. src/lib/tier-entitlements.ts — showUserAds correctly returns true for home-cook, but no app page asks the policy.
Why it matters
Prep Cook ($27.99/yr) exists to remove ads — that's the differentiator at the cheapest paid step. But Home Cook users never see ads on the pages they actually live on (/home, /recipes, recipe detail). The upgrade sells a relief from a friction the user has never felt. This is structurally the same problem as F1 (no checkout) — the entry-level conversion path is broken at the demand side rather than the supply side.
Recommendation
Add a single banner ad slot to free-tier authed pages — quiet (one unit, not a stack), one location per page. Extend AD_ENABLED_ROLES to include authenticated-home and authenticated-task; showUserAds() already gates correctly by tier. The Prep Cook pitch then becomes "buy back the clean view of the product you already know," which is the strongest possible framing for a removing-friction upgrade.
F3 Landing page doesn't sell the product
Severity
High
Where
src/routes/index.tsx
Why it matters
Three issues compound: (1) the hero says "CookBook" instead of "My CookBooks" — the brand name in the design system. (2) The three feature cards all describe browsing — Recipe Collection, Categories, Search & Filter — they're the same feature. (3) There is no mention of saving, importing, private cookbooks, printing, or sharing — the things behind a paid plan.
Recommendation
Rewrite hero with the brand name; rewrite features around the verbs the product enables (Save, Organise, Import, Print, Share); add a product screenshot or a recipe image; primary CTA → Start free (signup), secondary → Browse public recipes.
F4 Top-tier features are likely under-earning at the top tier
Severity
Medium
Where
src/lib/tier-entitlements.ts — canImport requires executive-chef; canCreatePrivate requires sous-chef.
Why it matters
Import is the most search-friendly acquisition hook in the entire product ("how do I import my recipes from X"). Locking it at $99.99/yr is defensible — it limits abuse and anchors the premium price — but worth deciding deliberately rather than by default. Same question for Private recipes at $59.99/yr. Tier 1 (Prep Cook) already has a clear job (remove ads); tiers 3 and 4 should each have one too.
Recommendation
Decide explicitly: is Import a premium feature (keeps it where it is) or an acquisition feature (pull it down to Sous Chef, anchor Executive Chef on family sharing or API)? Worth A/B testing if traffic allows. Independent of F2.
F5 No progressive paywall nudges
Severity
Medium
Where
src/routes/recipes/index.tsx — atRecipeLimit only fires at 100%.
Why it matters
The first time a user learns there's a limit is the moment they hit it. That's frustrating and reactive. Showing the ceiling earlier turns the limit into a planning signal, not a wall.
Recommendation
Three tiers of nudge: at 70%, a quiet inline notice on /recipes and the recipe form ("8 of 10 recipes used"). At 90%, a persistent banner with an "Upgrade" button. At 100%, the existing TierWall — keep it.
F6 Authed home is a links menu, not a dashboard
Severity
Medium
Where
src/routes/home.tsx
Why it matters
The first thing every returning user sees is two columns of generic links. There's no signal about their data — usage, recent activity, the cookbook they last opened — and no surface to drop an in-context upgrade nudge.
Recommendation
Reframe as a dashboard: usage card (recipes / cookbooks with progress bars), recent recipes (3–6 thumbnails), recent cookbook, a single contextual upgrade card when nearing limits or when a paid-only action was attempted recently.
F7 Account page's only upgrade path is a text link
Severity
Medium
Where
src/routes/account.tsx
Why it matters
The page does the right thing showing usage + next-tier preview, but the only call-to-action is a 14px underlined link reading "View pricing plans." If a user is on this page, they're already thinking about plans — the CTA should be a primary button with the specific upgrade ("Upgrade to Sous Chef — $3.99/mo").
Recommendation
Replace the text link with a primary CTA button targeted at the next tier. Below it, secondary link to "Compare all plans." If the next-tier preview is shown, include the specific delta benefit in the button label.
F8 Email verification blocks the first satisfying action
Severity
Medium
Where
requireVerifiedAuth on /recipes/new, /import, /change-tier.
Why it matters
The user signs up, sees a "check your email" screen, leaves the app to find the verification email, clicks the link, comes back. That's three context switches before they've felt any value. Verification matters, but not before first value.
Recommendation
Let an unverified user create one recipe locally / in a sandboxed state, then prompt verification when they try to save it. Or push verification to milestones — first share, first import, first paid action.
F9 Pricing page is sparse
Severity
Medium
Where
src/routes/pricing.tsx
Why it matters
The page renders 4 cards and stops. There's no monthly/annual toggle (both prices show side-by-side, which is harder to scan), no "most popular" treatment, no FAQ, no money-back guarantee, no social proof, no comparison table view.
Recommendation
Add an annual/monthly toggle (default annual since it's discounted). Visually emphasise the recommended tier. Below the cards: FAQ accordion (cancellation, billing, refunds), and either testimonials or a feature-by-feature comparison table.
F10 Registration form is dry
Severity
Low
Where
src/components/auth/RegisterForm.tsx
Why it matters
Four fields and a submit button. No reassurance ("free forever, no credit card"), no preview of what's behind the door, no testimonial. Conversion-rate impact is small per visitor but compounds because every paid customer passes through here.
Recommendation
Add a benefits sidebar next to the form on desktop: "What you get with a free account: save 10 recipes, build 1 cookbook, print anything, no credit card needed." Below submit: "Already have an account?" + "By signing up you agree to…" microcopy. Keep the form itself unchanged.

Severity
Low

Why it matters
The design system canonicalises the name as My CookBooks (capital C and capital B, joined). Trivial to fix; first impression matters.

Suggested order of work

Sequenced by what unblocks what — not by ease.

01

Unblock revenue (1 sprint)

Without this, the rest is decoration.

  • F1 — Wire Stripe Checkout into /change-tier. Webhook updates user.tier.
  • F11 — Fix the brand name on the landing hero (5-minute fix, ship it now).
02

Make the existing tier ladder felt (1–2 sprints)

Today's tier structure is largely sound — it just isn't being delivered in a way the user experiences.

  • F2 — Render ads on free-tier authenticated-home + authenticated-task roles. This is the single largest conversion lift available.
  • F9 — Redesign pricing page: monthly/annual toggle, "most popular" emphasis on Prep Cook, FAQ, comparison table.
  • F4 — Decide explicitly whether Import + Private stay at top tiers or move down. Independent decision from F2.
03

Move users through the funnel (1–2 sprints)

In-product surfaces that turn engagement into upgrade intent.

  • F5 — Progressive paywall nudges (70% / 90% / 100%).
  • F6 — Authed home becomes a dashboard with usage + recent activity + contextual upgrade card.
  • F7 — Account page primary CTA → next-tier upgrade button.
04

Polish acquisition (1 sprint)

Get more people through the top of the funnel and reduce drop-off in onboarding.

  • F3 — Landing page rewrite: hero, feature verbs, screenshot, primary CTA to signup.
  • F8 — Defer email verification past the first satisfying action.
  • F10 — Register page benefits sidebar.

What I'd want next

Two things are worth doing in parallel with implementation:

  • Analytics. Every recommendation above is a hypothesis until you can see drop-off between funnel stages. A simple event funnel (landing → signup → verify → first recipe → hit limit → pricing → checkout) will tell you which fixes earned the biggest lift.
  • Five-user interviews. Two free users, two paid users, one churned user. Cheap insurance against re-tiering in the wrong direction. The proposed tier structure above is opinionated — five conversations would either validate or kill it.

Happy to mock up any of the proposed UI changes — pricing page, dashboard, paywall nudges, landing — at fidelity, in the design system. Just point.
